    Designing an Identity System

    A journey to build trust and help people transact online securely. It's been more than a year since I joined the Identity team with a goal to create a platform for managing all your online identity at one place in a private secure and trustworthy way. We desired to create identity management tools give you the power to share and protect your data exactly the way you want. Controlling your personal information online doesn’t have to be hard. One of imporatant aspect for me has been to not to just define but redefine the problem as time passes and landscape changes.

    This is just half part of the story the second half which involves our API and a business platform to help our partners be able to integrate and provide a complete identity verification mechanism and also give users all the control as well as security.

    My role is to rally the team around user-centric design. I work across user testing, high-level UX design, UI design, and interactive & motion prototyping. Developed wireframes to support key pages and flows, communicating ideas in a credible manner, leveraging competitive research, conferences, and latest trends. Also, I was responsible for translating the product vision and the experience through diagrams and wireframes.

    The Problem & Goals

    Okay here is the scenario lets say Fiona is a 28-year-old Finance Associate and she uses Uber to ride to work, prefers to stay in Airbnb when she travels and also orders a lot of stuff via amazon. She has to prove that her identity separately in all these places and more but she doesn’t trust. She wants a portable and reusable digital identity platform which she can trust and securely authorize other services to use. Our findings indicated that people really wish for some sort of a digital passport verified as per government standards which they can use universally but also be able to manage and control their information. People often pointed out how difficult it is to verify themselves in each new services or website especially if it needs you to pull out a real state iD/passport or even providing SSN is not trustworthy.

    Our findings indicated that people really wished for some sort of a digital passport verified as per government standards which they can use universally but also be able to manage and control their information.

    A large part of my role is to also do user testing and analyze user feedback, analytics, and data to help the team identify opportunities for improvement in the experience of the product. Created interactive prototypes to simulate proposed functionality to aid in the design and development process. The challenge has always been to simulate the experience in the correct and cheap way to take the most out of the user research exercises.

    The Process & Workflow

    The initial research to look at how identity verification experience is designed currently in 3 spheres the Government, Financial institutions and Sharing Economy/Private Sector. We realized there were two sides to this problem, one where the end users felt the pain and wanted a better portable experience with their identity and second where a B2B partnership was needed with the private sector and govt. to educate and also help them integrate the solution. I talked to users and leadership to figure out what a product should be.

    We relied a lot on initial internal feedback to quickly adapt and iterate on interface design concepts.

    I usually break up product design life cycle into four stages. The first being Definition, I think honestly a lot of time companies or project, especially in larger companies, don’t spend enough time in the Definition Step. They think they define it and quickly move on to the problem-solving ideation stage. But I think the key is to know first is to identify the correct problem worth solving, how we know it’s successful and what the metrics are for success.

    With this in mind, we then started exploring what the iD Card experience could look like. For this, I went on to see what examples could help us design a more usable iD Card and created different iterations of it. Won over the difficult stakeholders by designing detailed wireframes for every screen and states.

    I think the key is to know first identify the correct problem worth solving, how we know it’s successful and what the metrics are for success.

    Early wireframing and prototyping allowed us to quickly eliminate the concepts that didn’t feel good enough, and to validate/test the ones that had more potential. I know it is important when to sweat the details and when to get a rough version out for prototyping.

    Experimentation is the new normal. We continually create simple things and launch them to figure out where the next big breakthrough will happen. The end result of this experimentation is that you compete with yourself.

    My goal is to focus on utility first and later on usability, as a product won't be successful even if we provide a great usable product.

    We focused on key features to make the experience more accessible and also simple. The first step was to bring some of the hidden settings to the forefront such as card visibility settings to everyone, just your connections or no one.

    Interactive Prototyping

    At this same time, I started learning Framer and wanted to apply it to design an interactive prototype for the iD Card. This was something that would delight the user and also make it look more like an identity card. I utilized prototypes a lot to simulate proposed functionality to aid in the design and development process. A part of the process was to also have the whole product flow clickthrough via Invision so that other team members can easily be aligned and collaborate via feedbacks.

    We also devised a way where we wanted to create a consistent visual design for the whole product and created a design styleguide to help all the team members be aligned.

    Working towards the North Star

    As we moved towards creating a portable, reusable and trustworthy digital passport, it was important to also make sure we paint clear and simple use cases. I started on creating use case card for our landing page to give users context and helping in scenarios to help users understand why/where they need to do identity verification and management online.

    This led to creating the landing page with the right message for our end users. My approach was to explain what is Identity.com, how does it work, what am I getting and why is it useful all on the landing page. Creating the landing page process was fun.

    Research & Internal Testing

    We designed our minimum viable product and wanted to know how people would perceive ID Card and if they would know when or why do they need to verify their identity. We quickly identified that data ownership was a big concern which also tied in with security. What the interface had to provide was to make them feel secure and in control. We created a “Verification” section which was more like a data vault, helping with more transparency around all their data all in one place.

    As we were focusing on our product, we got a chance to collaborate with New Zealand Govt. on a country wide concept on digital identities. New Zealand is one of the first countries to address the challenge of digital identity, starting with the launch of its government login service in 2007 determine future needs regarding technology, user experience, agency integration, and privacy. The collaboration aim is to result in a clear and realistic vision for the next generation of digital identity services in New Zealand. Our commitment was to help brainstorm creating a secure, privacy-friendly digital ecosystem, building trust online through advanced identity solutions and privacy-centric practices. Easy to use services and APIs that help people manage and protect their own identities, verify contacts, and build trusted teams at work. This collaboration helped us to learn a lot about how identity verification is important on a country scale, what could be the use cases and what challenges are they facing to introduce it to the citizen

    Lessons Learned & to the Future...

    I would again like to mention that the challenge for me has always been not to just define but redefine the problem as time passes and landscape changes every six months. Here are some of my thoughts around what I learned from my experience building this product.

    Define success at the start, mention your insights throughout and show how successful you were.
    Testing and validating assumptions early and repeatedly.
    We gathered as a team every other month, reflecting on our original goal of the design team and also engg. side helped us to be aligned and transparent.
    Being wrong is absolutely fine as long as you reflect on what went wrong and if given the chance what would you do to make it right.
    Ran surveys to quickly have real users able to express their understanding.
    The future relies on social online interactions based on trust and security.

    Building a Verification Platform

    As our lives become more digital, we're interacting with more people we've never met. We wanted to cater to this problem and allow companies and products to easily verify new members in their online community - so they and their users can build trust and confidence to interact more freely on their site.

    To achieve this goal we realized it is important to collaborate with companies, early stage startups in the sharing economy space to bring our vision of building an identity verification platform. We wanted to provide a great and simpler experience for the users as well as help companies to provide an industry-wide consistent verification standard and experience.

    Also in this space, sharing economy startups are focused on building great products and are great at it but they lack resource to build a verification platform, comply with legal standards and also prevent frauds from entering their community. Identity verification protects online marketplaces and communities by ensuring that the people signing up for the service are, in fact, who they say they are. It protects legitimate participants from potentially fraudulent ones

    Working towards this goal I figured out we need to create a very native and streamlined experience for verifying new members and also provide APIs and SDK to help developers to be able to easily integrate with us

    Identity verification protects online marketplaces and communities by ensuring that the people signing up for the service are, in fact, who they say they are. It protects legitimate participants from potentially fraudulent ones

    So what about the process?

    We started by exploring a lot of examples which involved a wizard like experience to complete tasks in different parallels. All the conversations always involved the Engg. team early on to collaborate and help along side the product team to understand some of the finer technical limitations as we dive into problem. We knew security and trust will be a big concern as our product deals with handling personal and private individual data in order to verify it. The goal for us was to focus on speed, simplicity and delight.

    The main question we were trying to answer was: How do you design an experience to optimize the workflow, to help users make the right decision without confusing them with too much information?

    Our aim was always to keep the solution simple and flexible. We started looking into OAuth as a possible candidate to authenticate the user securely and then verify their data. Companies like Google, Github and Facebook have implemented the experience and perfected the designs to cater to their use-cass pretty well. This would also help our users to transfer what they have experienced and learned from these popular user authentication systems.

    After a couple of initial Kickoff meetings with all the stakeholders and the product team in particular, I went on to create the initial task-based userflow. Once the team agreed upon the initial set of User Tasks, next was to device an easy and intuitive experience for users that would be initiated via a single button on our customer’s site to first authorize the user and then relay the relevant information to be verified. During this process one of the key part was to constantly pair up with the developers to understand certain complexities and constrains that could affect design and also help us create a quick MVP.

    One of the questions we always faced was what would be the behavior when someone clicks on “Verify Me” button. We looked into redirecting the users to identity.com sub domain, complete the steps and redirect them back. Another alternative was to open up a Pop-out window (e.g. Facebook Login) to do the verification step and would automatically dismiss once it's finished. The later experience also doesnt take the user away from what they were doing in the background and keep the context. Technically both were feasible, the Pop-out window was vital but challenging for the development team. Other approaches like Lightbox overlay were discarded due to security vulnerabilities.

    I came up with Initial wireframes for the verification steps, with multiple layout options to see what could work for us keeping in mind the experience should be adaptive for mobile. The goal was to create a simple layout with scaffolded steps for users so they are focused on one task at a time and are not burdened by the no. of steps as it could defer depending on attributes to be verified. I decided to try out 2-column layouts as well as 1-column layout as a part of wireframe brainstorming.

    The design decision was to frame content that helped users stay in the context of the task, providing just enough data points (eg. breadcrumb indicators and contextual content help) in the foreground to help make an informed decision.

    Less is More! I think the first page of the experience was very crucial, and so I started with comparative and competitive research on different aspects of the design; how to ask for private information, what clues create trust among users, how the content should be presented, does mashup of logo help users to understand data being shared ?

    The start of the experience should allow affordance for pre-existing Identity.com users to quickly verify and non-account holders to verify as a guest. So we created two buttons on that page.

    ▸ Users who already have Identity.com accounts are prompted to sign-in to their accounts. Then they complete any additional attribute verifications required and authorize the organization asking to access this information.

    ▸ Users who don’t have an Identity.com account are taken through the simple verification steps for the attributes specified and are then prompted to authorize the organization to access their information.

    Thereafter, I took the designs into the visual phase to create the whole experience and stitch it together as a click through mock helping in getting feedback from leadership and potential customers. I started with taking the 2-column design into more substantial mockups. The 2-column approach allowed us to ask for their personal detials and also help us pitch Identity.com and its brand with the left image layout.

    Feedback from stakeholders was that the images on split layout were distracting and we wanted a very focused design approach. So the next step was to iterate the designs so that the main focus as a user is to complete the step and move on to the next one. Also removing images would increase load times aswell as make the design more adaptive to different screen-sizes. Here is the next design approach with a more one-column content layout. It was one of the alternatives we explored in the wireframe stage.

    I wanted to create something that will solve a problem in identity verification experience, create something that is intuitive and easy to use by the users it is intended to serve.

    Thinking it as a System

    For me personally, this product was a way to learn to think of the solution from a designing a system perspective. It was not about just creating a static set of screen designs to verify user information but to think about all the edge cases in each scenario, all the errors that could occur and how to create a failsafe so that user doesn’t get stuck if the system is down or not responding.
    Customer support, FAQ and Help Center as ways where a user would fall back to find answer and help and we wanted to make sure we provide enough guide and assistance at each step.
    A lot of the times people would access certain customer’s site on the mobile web so we wanted to make sure our designs were mobile ready from day one.
    Thinking about our Customers who are verifying their user’s information with us also need insights, analytics, and tools to know what is happening between the flow.
    An important part of the experience is Email follow-ups with the users alerting them about any activity happening with their account or if someone else is verifying using their information. We knew our way of communicating with the user via email needs to be well thought off.

    Collaboration as a team sport

    The synergy between the designers and the developers is something I really enjoyed in this project a lot. Apart from pairing up with the front-end engineer, meaningful interactions with the backend developers working on the product architecture helped me understand certain difficult mechanics like OAuth, Javascript API, Browser localstorage and HistoryAPI for back etc. Collaborating with the Project Managers, Business Development team and Content Strategy team was immensely helpful for me to define and improve the product at each step.

    Prototyping & Usertesing

    This was not one of those projects where one can make wireframes and static mockups only, and I felt for this project to be successful prototyping it before it goes into development would be crucial to really be able to do testing with real users.

    You can see the click-through version of the prototype here Also, as our product is based on collecting personal information and verifying, it was important for us to see how users feel when they have to enter their personal information on another site. Will they trust us? Will they drop-off? Are they comfortable to enter their SSN or provide their Photo ID on a computer?

    We had soo many questions and so we curated a tasked based scenario to validate utility, usability and trust concerns regarding the flow. We did 10 in-person interviews and 18 online usertesting. Challenge was to mimic the prototype as the real product and also ask the right questions to bring out the cognitive affect states while users are interactive. The tests and interviews gave us lot of insights and critical product validation as well as some feedbacks which we wanted to cater to improve it for the next phase. I lead the usertesting Initiative from the design team paired with one Product Manager.

    I created a highlight reel of all the usertesting videos to showcase the voice and feedback of our users to the bigger team and stakeholders. It was a great hit as everyone got motivated after looking at the positive feedback and praised the simple intuitive experience.

    Challenges make us stronger

    The product had a lot of challenges are below are some which had me sweat.

    Usertesting the product needed the right fidelity and interactivity to replicate a real experience for our users.
    A product based on verifying user personal data is hard to simulate and test as people tend to become reluctant to test with their own data.
    As a designer learning new technical concepts on OAuth and Two Factor Authentication so as to build security & trust.
    Concerns on how do we measure trust among our users. How do we create a privacy by design product development approach to combine technology and empathy?

    Future Improvements

    We marked certain improvements coming from our tests and other feedback.

    ▸ Need of a better navigation/breadcrumbs indicator on the designs to better estimate remaining steps.

    ▸ Thinking of all failsafe scenarios and creating a customer support system

    ▸ Building trust with smartly placed visual cues like TRUSTe Certification logos and security lock icons as well as Just-in-time help tooltips during each step.

    ▸ Fixing scenarios where someone closes the tab in the middle, clicking browser back button to navigate to the previous step, does a user start everything from beginning or does it retain the already done steps? These were massively technical challenges to solve and improvements to these scenarios are a must.

    Building Verification API Platform

    For me, a well-designed documentation is a key to getting people excited about your project, especially for a developer focused product. It's a project in collaboration with a Technical Project Manager and a Front-end Engineer to create MVP for API Documentation complete our offering of Identity Verification solutions.

    The most important piece of UX for a developer product isn't the homepage or the sign up process or the SDK download. It's the API documentation! Who cares if your product has a great solution if no one understands how to use it. Documentation is a whole ecosystem of content that aims to teach users how to interact with your API.

    I started research on what are the best API documentation out there, talked with a lot of developers asking their favorite API products and documentation. What did they like about it? Why did they think it was easy and quick to integrate? How was the support and if the docs were updated? This helped us to quickly understand what really matters for a developer and product team to consider integrating with us as a customer.

    Some of the key aspect that I learned and implemented in the API Docs design are:

    Integration with just a few lines of code to keep it simple
    Beautiful and clean typography and nice layout with emphasis on content
    A gentile Quickstart / Overview
    Minimize clicking by having a single page long scroll documentation
    Providing ample reference or tutorials to help developers with a guided approach.
    Mobile rendering should be accessible

    Other essential interface elements that I focused on in the API Docs design:

    Easy hierarchical Navigation system
    Colored syntax for better code comprehension and readability.
    Multiple programming language support if possible

    Customer Dashboard

    A vital component of our API & SDK offering is to also empower our customers with analytics tools and insights to inform of a dashboard to monitor, manage and learn post integration with the API.

    The goal here is to create an Insights view inform of a dashboard to track and gain information on their user’s verification. We also added certain modules to show API usage and billings in a clean layout.

    I started work on the dashboard picking the brains of our business development team and potential customer’s need. This help to design certain parts of the Dashboard based on the inputs. Especially a section which tracked how many people attempted to verify themselves vs how many successfully verified (a very important stat from customer’s perspective). After numerous iterations, back and forth with the stakeholders, we decided on the final design for the Dashboard.

    The portal also allowed the developers to manage API application and a section for admins to assign roles (i.e. read-only or edit). For me, this project was a challenge from data prioritization and visualization, as we started with a lot of information to be displayed but went on with an approach to remove those parts which were not useful and keep only those which would be essential on a daily basis.

    Final Thoughts

    ID API & Dashboard projects helped me learn a lot on how effectively I can collaborate with multi-disciplinary teams, especially engineering and content teams.
    Customers care a lot about security and reliability, and always want it to be a part of the design thought process from the get go.
    A well-defined project from the inception helps us to be more efficient in implementation.
